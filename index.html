Audit rates by bracket over time.
Budget erosion story: IRS enforcement funding fell from $5.5B (2010) to $4.5B (2016) in real terms — a sustained 18% cut. The result: the highest-income filers saw their audit probability drop from 18.38% to 2.48% in a single decade. The 2022 Inflation Reduction Act reversed course with $80B in new funding, but rates remain well below 2010 levels.

Federal audit rate by total positive income — Tax Year 2022 IRS Data Book Table 17
2022 audit rates: under $25k 0.61%, $25-50k 0.26%, $50-75k 0.21%, $75-100k 0.20%, $100-200k 0.22%, $200-500k 0.38%, $500k-1M 0.63%, $1M-5M 1.10%, $5M-10M 2.35%, over $10M 4.40%.
The U-shaped curve explained: Low-income filers (<$25k) face elevated rates primarily due to Earned Income Tax Credit compliance checks — the EITC has historically high error rates. The $75k–$200k range is the statistical safest zone. Above $500k, rates climb steeply as the IRS focuses on high-complexity, high-dollar returns where additional assessments are largest.
EITC audit concentration: A significant portion of <$25k audits are correspondence audits (not field audits) specifically targeting EITC eligibility — a very different audit experience than a high-income field examination. The raw rate overstates the "severity" of low-income audit risk.

Key sensitivity finding: For a self-employed filer at $200k AGI, adding a reported business loss pushes the risk score from 67.7 (Elevated) to 70.8 (High) — a 14% increase from a single additional factor. The model shows compounding effects are most pronounced in the $150k–$600k range where marginal factor changes have the largest impact on examination probability.
key findings
56% rate collapse (2010–2020)
Sustained IRS budget cuts reduced the overall audit rate from 1.11% to 0.29% — the lowest in modern history. High-income rates fell even faster, from 18.38% to 2.48% for filers over $10M.
IRA recovery underway
The 2022 Inflation Reduction Act allocated $80B to IRS enforcement. By 2022, rates had recovered to 0.49% — real progress, but still less than half of 2010 levels. Full recovery is expected through 2025–2027.
Self-employment: 2.5x multiplier
Schedule C filers face approximately 2.5x the audit probability of W-2 filers at the same AGI — the single largest individual risk factor. Cash-intensive businesses add another 1.4x on top.
Charitable deduction thresholds
Charitable giving above 5% of AGI adds a 15% risk premium. Above 10%, the premium jumps to 35%. Above 20%, it reaches 80%. These thresholds reflect IRS DIF score break points documented in TIGTA reports.
